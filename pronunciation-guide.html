A Quick Guide to Pronunciation

Mastering the Pure Greek Vowels

One of the most noticeable differences for an English speaker is the limited number of vowel sounds in Greek. While English is full of diphthongs—complex sounds where one vowel slides into another, like the "o-oo" sound at the end of the word "go"—Greek relies on five "pure" vowels that never change their shape: ah [α], eh [ε], ee [ι], o [ο], and oo [υ].

When you see transliterations like "tahn" for ταν or "soh" for σω, the added "h" serves as a visual anchor. It reminds you to keep the sound clipped and stable. If you pronounce ταν like the English word "tan," or σω like the English word "so," you are adding extra "glide" sounds that don't exist in Greek. Treat every vowel as a single, static note.

Consonants: Dental vs. Alveolar

In English, we are "alveolar" speakers. When we pronounce "l," "n," "t," or "d," our tongue hits the fleshy ridge just above our teeth. To speak Greek correctly, you must shift your tongue forward. For the letters λ, ν, and τ, your tongue should press against the back of your upper teeth (this is called a lingua-dental position).

Additionally, the letter δ (delta) is always "voiced," behaving like the hard "th" in "that" or "those." To keep it distinct from the "breathier" sound in "theory" or "thin," we use "dh" in our text. Finally, always give your "r" sounds a quick, energetic roll or tap—think of the "r" in the word "three" rather than the long, American "r" in "car."

The Guttural "Shift" of Chi and Gamma

The two most distinct sounds, χ (chi) and γ (gamma), act like chameleons—their sound changes based on the vowel that follows them.

  • The Deep Shift: Before "ah," "oh," and "oo" sounds, these letters are guttural. χ sounds like a raspy breath (the "ch" in "loch"), and γ sounds like a gargled, soft "g."
  • The Sharp Shift: Before "ee" and "eh" sounds, they move forward in the mouth. χ becomes a smooth, airy hiss, and γ sounds like a textured, heavy "y."

Subtle Airflow and "Flicker" Sounds

In English, we often "aspirate" our letters, meaning we release a puff of air when we say words starting with "p" or "t." In Greek, π and τ are pronounced "softly," meaning you should cut off that burst of air entirely.

Lastly, there is a melodic nuance to watch for: when κ, γ, or χ appear before an "eh" sound, the speaker should introduce a nearly invisible "ee" flicker. It is a momentary transition that bridges the consonant to the vowel, adding a authentic liturgical texture to the pronunciation.
